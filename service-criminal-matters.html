About Criminal Matters

Criminal law deals with acts that are considered offences against the state or society — such as cheating, fraud, theft, assault, harassment, and more. Under Indian law (IPC / Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita), these offences are punishable with imprisonment, fines, or both.

When should you act? If you have been a victim of cheating, criminal breach of trust, fraud, threats, physical assault, or harassment, you should file a complaint immediately. Delay can result in evidence loss and makes investigation harder. You have the right to register an FIR at any police station — and if the police refuse, you can approach the Judicial Magistrate directly.

Common criminal matters include: Cheating & fraud (Section 420 IPC), Criminal breach of trust (Section 406), Assault & threat (Sections 351–354), Extortion (Section 383), Forgery (Section 463), Dowry harassment (Section 498A), and cases under the BNS 2023.

2

Eligibility / Who Can File

Any person who is a victim of a criminal offence can file a complaint. For cognizable offences (like cheating, assault), the police must register an FIR. For non-cognizable offences, a complaint is filed before a Magistrate.

  • Any Indian citizen — victim of a criminal act
  • Companies, banks, and institutions (through authorised representative)
  • Third party who witnessed a cognizable offence
  • Minors — complaint filed through parent or guardian

Note: For private criminal complaints (non-cognizable), a complaint under Section 200 CrPC (now BNSS) must be filed before the Judicial Magistrate within the applicable limitation period.

Required Documents

  • Aadhaar Card / Government ID Proof
  • Written complaint / FIR copy (if already filed)
  • Evidence of the offence (screenshots, photos, videos)
  • Bank statements / transaction records
  • Correspondence (WhatsApp, email, SMS)
  • Witness names and contact details
  • Medical report (if assault/injury)
  • Forged documents / fake agreements (if any)
  • Cheque / demand draft (if cheating via payment)
  • Police complaint acknowledgement (if applicable)
  • Previous legal notices sent (if any)
  • FIR copy from police station (if already filed)

An FIR is filed at the police station for cognizable offences (e.g., cheating, assault) and triggers a police investigation. A private complaint is filed before a Magistrate for non-cognizable offences and results in the court taking cognizance and summoning the accused.
If you fear arrest in a criminal case, you can apply for Anticipatory Bail under Section 438 CrPC before the Sessions Court or High Court. Our advocate will file an urgent anticipatory bail application with appropriate grounds.

Benefits of Taking Criminal Action

🚔

Police Investigation

A registered FIR triggers an official police investigation, search, arrest, and recovery of stolen assets.

⚖️

Criminal Accountability

The accused faces prosecution, conviction, and imprisonment — powerful deterrent against wrongdoing.

💰

Compensation Under Section 357

Courts can award compensation to victims directly from fines imposed on the convicted accused.

🛡️

Protection Orders

Courts can issue restraining orders against the accused to protect you from further harm or intimidation.

🔍

Asset Attachment

Property and bank accounts of the accused can be attached during investigation to prevent disposal of assets.

📣

Public Record & Deterrence

A criminal conviction creates a public record and serves as a deterrent against repeat offences.
