About Cheque Bounce Cases

A cheque bounce occurs when a banker returns a cheque unpaid due to insufficiency of funds, signature mismatch, stopped payment, or other reasons. Under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, a cheque bounce is a criminal offense punishable by imprisonment up to two years, a fine up to double the cheque amount, or both.

Strict Timelines: Cheque bounce cases are governed by very strict legal timelines. Once a cheque is bounced, you must send a formal statutory legal notice to the drawer within 30 days of receiving the memo. The drawer is given 15 days to pay. If they fail to pay, you must file the court case within 30 days thereafter.

Purpose of Section 138: The law is designed to promote the credibility of cheques in business transactions. The criminal nature of the case provides immense leverage, forcing drawers to settle outstanding dues quickly to avoid jail time.

2

Eligibility / Who Can File

To file a criminal complaint under Section 138 NI Act, the following conditions must be met:

  • The cheque must have been issued towards the discharge of a legally enforceable debt or liability (not as a gift or charity).
  • The cheque must have been presented to the bank within its validity period (usually 3 months).
  • The bank must have returned the cheque with a return memo showing dishonour (insufficient funds, etc.).
  • The payee must have made a written demand (legal notice) for payment within 30 days of receiving the memo.
  • The drawer must have failed to make payment within 15 days of receiving the notice.
3

Required Documents

  • Original Bounced Cheque
  • Original Bank Return Memo (showing reason for dishonour)
  • Copy of the 30-day Statutory Legal Notice sent to the drawer
  • Postal Dispatch Receipt & Delivery Acknowledgment (or tracking report)
  • Proof of debt liability (Invoices, Ledger, Loan Agreement, or Promissory Note)
  • Bank statement showing the transaction attempt
  • Identity Proof of the complainant

Benefits of Taking Legal Action

👮

Criminal Liability

Threat of jail time makes debtors settle rapidly compared to standard civil recovery suits.

💰

Double Compensation

Court can order payment of up to twice the cheque face value to cover interest and harassment.

⏱️

Fast-Track Courts

Many jurisdictions have special courts specifically for cheque bounce cases, speeding up trials.

📝

Interim Compensation

Under Section 143A, the court can order the drawer to pay up to 20% of the cheque amount as interim relief during the trial.

🏢

Corporate Liability

If a company issued the cheque, director level officials are made co-accused, forcing board-level action.

🤝

High Settlement Rate

Over 80% of cheque bounce cases end in a settlement during the initial hearings or notice stage.
